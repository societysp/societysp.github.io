Structure:

Dead on Arrival

What can we say of structuralism except that its arrival on American shores was equally the proclamation of its death. . . DOA, dead on arrival. More than just a cliche retelling of historical events, this death is itself expressive of the problematic of ‘structure.’ It evokes the precarious and disoriented temporality which pertains to structure. The supposed heralder of structure’s death, Jacques Derrida, was well aware of this question. The opening lines of Derrida’s Writing and Difference begin,

“If it recedes one day, leaving behind its works and signs on the shores of our civilization, the structuralist invasion might become a question for the historian of ideas, or perhaps even an object. But the historical would be deceived if he to this pass: by the very act of considering the structuralist invasion as an object he would forget its meaning and forget what is at stake, first of all is an adventure of vision, a conversion of the way of putting questions to any object posed before us, to historical objects in particular.” (Derrida 1978:3)

As Derrida notes near the end of the quote, structure complicates the taking of any historical object altogether. The standard reading of classical structuralism supposes that its failure was its inability to accommodate diachronic processes. Structure is considered to be founded upon the very alienation of temporality from its concerns. The examples served are Saussure’s distinction between langage and parole—the system and a particular instantiation of it—or his theoretical turn from exploring language as a primarily historical development to its internal set of self-functioning rules and possibilities. In Fredric Jameson’s classic survey of structuralism, The Prison-House of Language, the opposition between synchrony v.s. diachrony plays as the central motif. The death of structure though cannot just mean a return to older, former understanding. The advent of structure brings about a whole new ‘logical time.’

Regardless, it cannot but appear as anachronistic and antiquated to again be speaking of the concept of structure. In contrast to this prevailing opinion though, we assert that the moment of structure has not yet arrived. If Hegel tells us that philosophy arrives late to comprehend the moment, structure is only just arriving. Empirically of course, structure came and went, but it has not yet been properly conceived in its actuality.
