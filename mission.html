For a Revival of Structure

“If it recedes one day, leaving behind its works and signs on the shores of our civilization, the structuralist invasion might become a question for the historian of ideas, or perhaps even an object. But the historical would be deceived if he to this pass: by the very act of considering the structuralist invasion as an object he would forget its meaning and forget what is at stake, first of all is an adventure of vision, a conversmailation of the way of outing questions to any object posed before us, to historical objects—his own—in particular.”

Jacques Derrida, Structure, Sign, and Play

Structuralism—the intellectual movement which sought to conceptualize and take as its basis the notion of structure—is all but dead today; or rather, it is undead. An idea, a movement, which at one time inspired vigour and vitality in the intellectual life of France and beyond now sees its corpus as corpses. But was it ever really a time lived? Was it dead from the start?

Regardless, it cannot but appear as anachronistic and antiquated to again be speaking of the concept of structure. In contrast to this prevailing opinion though, we assert that the moment of structure has not yet arrived. If Hegel tells us that philosophy arrives late to comprehend the moment, structure is only just arriving. Empirically of course, structure came and went, but it has not yet been properly conceived in its actuality.

This is not because we have grown past the time of structuralism, but it was a time which could never get started. The announcement of anything like the program of structuralism was doubly the proclamation of its death, of its supersession.

Structuralism is most associated with the development of French thought in the humanities and social sciences during the 1950’s to 1970’s. These dates are rough at best; whenever one begins to search for a definitive timeframe, the frame itself quickly begins to expand in both directions.

Jacques Derrida

Lacanian Schema

We generally subsume formalism under structuralism where we recognize in structuralism as making the step beyond the limits of the literary and consider all aspects of social life as text. One of the operating principles of Russian formalism was to distinguish the literary from the everyday. This is central to the formalist concept of “defamiliarization.”

The thesis would be this: Structuralism is the re-actualization of the development of German idealist thought from Kant to Hegel. Key to this is the evaluation that structuralism is still an ongoing development, and further, that we have not yet made the Hegelian move.
